The Age of Steam and Steel

1850 - 1920

A crucial period of technological growth occurred between the years 1850 and 1920. This era saw the perfection of the Bessemer Process which finally made large-scale steel production inexpensive and efficient.

Before this advancement, iron was the primary building material, but steel‚Äôs greater strength-to-weight ratio allowed for the construction of taller structures and more powerful machines. The Bessemer process involved blowing air through molten pig iron to remove impurities and it revolutionized manufacturing.

This industrial momentum also led to innovations in transportation.

Click here to see the early train models that reduced travel time across the continent.

The use of coal as a primary fuel source propelled these factories and vehicles, though it also created a heavy environmental toll. It is important to remember that all this progress was built on the backs of a growing industrial labor force and their families. Go to the section on factory conditions.

The final technological leap of the period was the widespread adoption of electricity, moving industry beyond steam power.

See images of the first electric lights in the city.

This immense output transformed global economies, creating the modern industrial world. However, the rapidity of change often masked deep social inequalities. The relentless pursuit of efficiency prioritized machinery over human welfare, leading to long working hours and dangerous factory environments. The shift from rural life to crowded urban centers also placed enormous strain on public services.

The shift from steam to electricity represented a profound change in how power was generated and distributed. While the internal combustion engine was still developing, the convenience and efficiency of electric lighting, motors, and streetcars fundamentally reshaped city life and set the stage for the next century of innovation.

The Bessemer Process was a revolutionary method for mass-producing steel by blowing air through molten pig iron to remove impurities like carbon and silicon. This innovation drastically reduced steel production costs and accelerated industrial growth during the late 19th century.

The industrial labor force refers to the large group of workers who powered the factories, railroads, and construction sites of the Industrial Revolution. Often working long hours in unsafe conditions, these laborers were essential to industrial progress but rarely shared equally in its rewards.
